AT THE CROSSROADS
BETRAYAL

   by Pastor  Martha-Jean Fitch
March 1, 2020
TEXT:  John 13:21-30

 

 

(This message begins with a sermon dialogue with Judas Iscariot – Produced by Creative Communications for the Parish and written by Ted Schroeder ((with edits by Pastors Greg and

Martha-Jean)). www.creativecommunications.com)

 

            Our Scripture for today comes from the gospel of John, the 13th chapter.   I invite you to get your Bibles out and follow along as we read verses 12-30.  Here begins the reading:

 

21 Now Jesus was deeply troubled, and He exclaimed, “I tell you the truth, one of you will betray me!” 22 The disciples looked at each other, wondering whom He could mean. 23 The disciple Jesus loved was sitting next to Jesus at the table.   24 Simon Peter motioned to Him to ask, “Who’s He talking about?” 25 So that disciple leaned over to Jesus and asked, “Lord, who is it?”  26 Jesus responded, “It is the one to whom I give the bread I dip in the bowl.” And when He had dipped it, He gave it to Judas, son of Simon Iscariot. 27 When Judas had eaten the bread, Satan entered into him. Then Jesus told him, “Hurry and do what you’re going to do.” 28 None of the others at the table knew what Jesus meant. 29 Since Judas was their treasurer, some thought Jesus was telling him to go and pay for the food or to give some money to the poor. 30 So Judas left at once, going out into the night.

 

            May God bless the reading of His word.

 

 

PASTOR:      Crossroads. They are there for all of us. We may move along from day to day easily, walking in the sunshine, enjoying the view, feeling at peace. And then it comes. A crossroad. A choice point. There we stand, frozen to the spot. Which way now. Right? Left? Straight? What waits down each of those choices? Which way does our heart call us to go? Which makes sense? Which is God’s way?  The answers aren’t easy. A crossroad can bring overwhelming and unnerving spiritual pain. And it can bring us to our knees. It can even bring us to destruction.

Today, we have an expert on the crossroad of temptation. We are going to interview the one who has become identified with the sin of betrayal. His name is a watchword for the failure of loyalty, failure of will, failure itself. We are going to interview Judas Iscariot.

 

(Judas appears. He is disheveled in appearance

 He appears stressed and troubled.)

 

PASTOR:  Welcome, Judas. I know this must be difficult for you, but we hope to understand you better. Can you tell us something about yourself?

 

JUDAS: Tell you? Why should I tell you? You all know about me. Everyone knows about me. My name is spoken often, too often, and it always is with condemnation.

PASTOR:   We know. We know the story. But we want to understand. It seems amazing to us that you would betray Jesus for a few pieces of silver, even when you were warned. How could that have happened?

 

JUDAS:     You know, when I look back on that night in the upper room and in the garden, I ask myself the same question. But by then I had already decided to take the money and betray Jesus. I had already gotten to the crossroad and made the choice. And the choice turned out to be a disaster.

 

PASTOR:      But didn’t you know it would turn out that way? Didn’t you know that betraying Jesus would lead to his arrest and even his death? If you knew that, how could you do it?

 

JUDAS:         It all seems too easy to you, cut and dried when you read the story. But there was more to it. I really didn’t know it would turn out that way. I made the choice to accept the money. I needed the money. And Jesus kept promising that he was going to set up a kingdom and that we would be rewarded. I wanted to be there when the rewards came. But it was taking so long. And he kept talking about suffering and dying. I didn’t need that. I needed money.

 

PASTOR:      But how could you make that choice?

 

JUDAS:         I had convinced myself, or Satan had convinced me, that it wouldn’t be so bad. After all, Jesus wasn’t exactly hiding. He rode into Jerusalem in a grand procession, appeared in the temple and on the streets. What would my marking him do? He could escape easily. He had done it before. My act was nothing. I thought he would just disappear through the crowd as He had in the past.

 

PASTOR:  Buy why? Was the money so important?

 

JUDAS:         It seemed like it at the time.   And I thought I might be doing Jesus a favor and getting Him in a place where He could begin the revolution that would overcome the Romans and start His new kingdom. I thought I was doing the right thing. At least that is what Satan was whispering to me.

 

PASTOR:  You thought that the betrayal would turn out for the good?

 

JUDAS:         That is what I convinced myself. Listen. Everyone thinks that they understand how Satan works, that he is kind of there trying to get us to do bad things. Not true. Much more, he is trying to make us believe that the evil that lies before us at the crossroad is actually good, that it will all work out for the good. He is not just trying to make us do wrong, but to think wrong.

 

PASTOR:      We thought temptation was to sin. What are you telling us?

 

JUDAS:  Every sin starts with a twisted thought. Look at Jesus when He was tempted by Satan; those things that Satan was trying to get Jesus to do were not wrong in themselves. Jesus could have turned stones to bread and not have sinned, but Satan was tempting Him like he tempts us: If you are the Son of God, if you really are, you can use your power for yourself. Sin starts in the mind, in the will, in the choice.

 

PASTOR:    But we are not like Jesus or even like you. Our choices are simpler, more day to day. We don’t betray Jesus.

 

JUDAS:         No? A businessman faces a choice to cut the quality on his products. No one will be hurt, he thinks. He is just doing good business. And he makes the choice. He convinces himself that what he is doing is not wrong. Or someone says that getting back at another person who has caused hurt is a good thing to do. It will teach them a lesson, we think. The sin begins in the thoughts that lead us to excuse our bad choices, to justify what we are doing. And then we find ourselves down the wrong road—in the middle of the terrible outcome of our choice. And then ...

 

PASTOR:   And then?

 

JUDAS:         Well, for me it was too late. When I realized what the outcome of my choice was, when I saw Jesus arrested and condemned, and not starting His kingdom like I wanted - I could not face myself, my future, my God. But for you, when you find yourselves down the wrong road, facing the consequences of your choice, then is the time to turn back. Repent. Turn around. Turn away from the bad choice. Turn to the one whom you have betrayed.

 

PASTOR:  Why didn’t you do that?

 

JUDAS:         I thought my sin was unforgiveable. I had betrayed the chosen one of God. I thought it was too late for me. But it is never too late—never too late to turn. If you learn anything from me, it should be this: No matter what the sin, no matter the terrible outcome of your choice, no matter how great the disaster, it is never too late. It is never too late to come to the cross, come to the broken Savior, come to the empty tomb and be made whole.

 

PASTOR:     Even when the sin is a huge as a mountain?

 

JUDAS:         Even when the pain is so deep it cannot be spoken, even when the separation from God seems too far, when the damage seems too severe, it is never too late. You can be assured that these words are true: “If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness” (1 John 1:9). That’s all. All of it. Everything. I wish I had turned.

 

PASTOR:      Thank you, Judas, for your good words. We did not expect them from you, but we have learned from you that we can always turn to our Lord and receive his forgiveness.

 

            You know as I look at the life of Judas, I think the real tragedy is that he probably died – hanging on a tree – before Jesus died on the cross of Calvary.   Yes – Judas was sorry for what he did – but he didn’t go to Jesus to ask for forgiveness.   He wasn’t there.   He wasn’t at the foot of the cross to hear those incredible words of Jesus, when He said, “Father, forgive them, for they don’t know what they are doing.”

 

            You know, I believe that there would have been forgiveness for Judas – if he had just returned to Jesus.  Instead he suffered alone, in despair, unwilling to believe he could be forgiven, until his remorse drove him to take his own life.   In doing that, he missed the whole reason for the crucifixion.   Jesus died to free us from the power of sin.   And no one is outside His mercy and grace.   There is no sin so great that God’s grace is not greater still.    As Corrie ten Boom said, “There is no pit so deep that God’s love is not deeper still.”  

 

            But Judas believed the lie – he thought his sin was too big.   He thought he could never be forgiven – that his life was not worth redeeming.   And oh – how many people believe this lie even today.   They die in despair, not knowing the incredible grace and peace that comes from trusting in Jesus – asking for His forgiveness and living in His grace.

 

            Dr. Tom Long writes of the unforgettable story of visiting a small prayer chapel at a church in Jacksonville, Florida.  Many works of art are there, including the painting that portrays Jesus washing the feet of His disciples.  And a likeness of the famous Da Vinci paining of the Last Supper was there.

 

            But the scene that captured Dr. Long’s mind the most featured a life-sized painting of Jesus, with a sign on it from Matthew 11:28-30 which says, “Come unto me all that are heavy laden, and I will give you rest.   Take my yoke upon you and learn of Me; for I am meek and lowly in heart; and you shall find rest for your souls.   My yoke is easy and my burden in light.”

 

            Twelve upholstered chairs had been placed in a semi-circle facing the painting to provide a place for people to focus on it.  Each chair was labeled with the name of one the twelve disciples.  You could go and sit in the chair of Peter or Matthew or John and sit and reflect upon this portrait of Jesus calling you to come to Him.  And, yes, among those 12 chairs there was a Judas chair—a chair inscribed with the name of that the one who betrayed Jesus with a kiss that led to Jesus’ arrest, trial, and crucifixion.

 

            Dr. Long was struck by the fact that among all the 12 chairs that showed the most wear – the chair where people had sat the most often – was the chair marked “Judas”.

 

            At first, he was a bit confused by that.   Why would people pick that chair?  He didn’t like to think of himself as “Judas”.   He thought, “My little sins don’t compare with Judas - his huge sin of betrayal.”   But the more he thought about it, he decided that the people who wore out that “Judas chair” in the prayer chapel really had a deeper understanding of their sin – and of the incredible grace and mercy of Jesus than he did.

 

            We have the power to be Judas – to betray Jesus with our lips and our hearts and actions.   Or we can turn around and come to the Savior and be made whole.  And what glory it is to know that Jesus is that Savior of all humankind.   He came to offer Himself as a perfect sacrifice for our sins and to make a way for us to be at peace with God.

            He shows us the power of the resurrection and the victory over death as we put our faith and trust in Him.   And the power of the Holy Spirit that can give us new life each and every day!

 

            Judas missed it - he missed the forgiveness of Christ, the peace of God and the power of the Holy Spirit, but today I want you to know that you don’t have to. All that Judas missed is available to you this morning. There is a wideness in God’s mercy and there is nothing so bad that you have done that Jesus can’t forgive you. But just as Judas had to make the choice for himself, you will have to make the choice for yourself.   Will you choose Christ?   What will be your answer?
