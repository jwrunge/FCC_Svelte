SHELTER
THIRSTING FOR GOD

  by Pastor Martha-Jean Fitch
July 12, 2020
TEXT:   Psalm 63

 

            David Jeremiah, in his book Shelter in God, tells the story of Mary Mallon who was born in Ireland in 1869 and then migrated to the United States.   She soon started working as a cook for wealthy families in New York City.   But there was something unusual about Mary.   Medical authorities discovered that wherever she worked, members of the family would come down with typhoid fever – and some even died.   Mary was completely asymptomatic and did not believe that the illnesses and deaths had anything to do with her.1

 

            Eventually, in 1907, she was quarantined in isolation in a New York clinic for three years.   She went through a lot of medical testing and was one of the first of many people to be diagnosed as an “asymptomatic carrier” of typhoid fever.  When she was released, she agreed to stop working as a cook and to be very careful to prevent infecting others.

 

            In 1915, typhoid fever broke out in a Hospital, affecting 25 workers and killing two.   The epidemic was traced back to the hospital cook, who people had nicknamed “Typhoid Mary”.   Little did they know at the time, it was Mary Mallon who had changed her name to Mary Brown.   Mary had broken her promise to stay out of the kitchen and in the end, 51 cases of typhoid could be traced to Mary – 3 of whom died.  This time she was put in forced quarantine and she died under quarantine 23 years later.   Ever since reading her story I have wondered what her life must have been like.   How did she cope and endure that isolation and pain?2

 

            Over the years, there have been many “forced” quarantines – but here in the year 2020, we have a new term called “self-quarantine” – and “social distancing” – keeping six feet apart. It is all in an effort to help us all stay healthy and safe and to make sure we aren’t an asymptomatic carrier.

 

 I don’t know about you – but this pandemic quarantine has gone on so much longer than I would have ever dreamed it would have.   These times could almost be called wilderness years – when we have had so much uncertainty and anxiety, isolation and fear and loneliness.   How do we handle it all?

 

            Through our series on the book of Psalms, we have seen how David, who wrote many of the Psalms, had so many of the same struggles and feelings we have.   In today’s Psalm, we find David in his wilderness times.  As he cries out to God, he leads us through his despair to his devotion and joy and the hope he has in God, his helper.

 

            The inscription for Psalm 63 reads that David wrote this psalm when he was in the Judean wilderness.   But when and why was he there?   Most scholars believe it is written when David is on the run from his son Absalom.     David had been king for several years and fought many battles, but it was his own son Absalom who broke his heart by leading a conspiracy against him.   Absalom wanted to steal the throne away from David.   When David hears about it, he chooses to flee to the wilderness in order to avoid the confrontation and potentially destroy Jerusalem.  He “self-quarantined”, if you will, so that he could sort out the mess his kingdom – and his family – was in.

 

            Have you ever known that kind of pressure – and pain?   Maybe you were struggling with family unrest or an unstable marriage – or maybe it was a time of financial failure or walking through a devastating health crisis.   Or maybe it is just being in this wilderness time of uncertainty of the pandemic.   You find you self in the middle of a muddle – I heard it described that way recently.3   You know what a muddle is?   It is being in a disordered or confusing state.   What do you do when you are “in the middle of a muddle”?  Let’s hear how David responds, by hearing his prayer in Psalm 63.

 

KIM BARTON READS PSALM 63

 

            As I hear Psalm 63, I picture David standing in the hot desert rocks of Judean hills – shoulders slumped as he is exhausted physically, and emotionally – and yes, even spiritually.   He is as dry and empty as the landscape he is looking at.   His family has turned against him and many of his subjects have rejected him – and he doesn’t know what hope there is for his kingdom.   But notice, he doesn’t cry out for all the things he has lost – nor does he beg for God to change everything and end his suffering.   Rather, he realizes that only God can heal the brokenness in His life – so He begins his prayer by saying that he is seeking God earnestly.   He is not just saying just a simple prayer –no, he is yearning for God and seeking him with all his heart, soul, and mind.   His deepest need is to be in close communication and relationship with God.   So, his soul longs for – and can only be satisfied by – a close, deep relationship with God.

 

            In longing for that closeness with God, David starts remembering times when he was so aware of God’s glory and power – and that was when he was worshiping God in the sanctuary of the tabernacle.   I think he is longing to have that time of worship again.   But think about it - he is cut off from the sanctuary and away from the worshipping people.  And his soul is yearning to have that worship – that time of prayer and connection with God’s people.

 

I must admit – I can relate to that feeling – and perhaps you can too.   I have so missed having corporate worship in our sanctuary.   Hearing the songs of faith being sung and feeling the presence of God in our midst as we share in communion together.   There have been so many times, I have felt and known God’s power and glory when I have been worshiping in the sanctuary.  There is something holy and reviving about coming together to worship God. 

 

But as we are in this wilderness time, when we are apart and away from our church family and worship in the sanctuary, we can choose to do what David did – and that is to worship where he was – and to praise God’s holy name and remember who He is.  

 

In verses 1 through 6, David shares all about His confidence in God and how he will praise God with all that is within him.    Even though his life was falling apart, he chose to praise God and focus on Him - and that praise changes everything.

 

David Jeremiah says this about choosing to praise God: “In the midst of the lonely desert road, what are you to do?   Praise God with every part of your body, mind, and spirit.   Learn to praise God regardless of your personal circumstances, and you’ll see miracles occur.   You heart and mind will be renewed.   Your perspective will widen panoramically.   And your attitude toward God will never be the same.”4  You will start to find hope and strength and power.   God will fill you up with His living water – and you will begin to have an inner satisfaction - as David says in verse 5.  Instead of being empty and dry – you will be filled up  with the Spirit of God, which can bring an inner calm and peace that passes all understanding.   The best way to change an anxious and worried heart is to have a praising and grateful heart.

 

Now I imagine that while David was in the wilderness, he knew times of tossing and turning in his sleep.  There were times I am sure that he woke up worrying about his situation and how he’d ever work out his problems with Absalom.   But instead of dwelling on all that – look what he does in verses 6 & 7.   He once again turns to worshiping God and remembering who he is.   He says: “I lie awake thinking of you, meditating on you through the night.   Because you are my helper, I sing for joy in the shadow of your wings.”

 

I know there are many of you that have trouble sleeping – and for a variety of reasons.   Some of it may be a physical condition that causes insomnia – but others of you have expressed how anxiety about our world and its uncertainties and unknowns have caused you to have some sleepless nights.   I want to encourage you to do as David did – and focus more on the Savior than your situation.     Remind yourself who He is and what He has done in your life.   Instead of counting sheep (or counting all your worries and fears) – focus on the Shepherd and count the ways He has blessed you.

 

You’ve heard me say how I continually use my A-B-C’s when I wake up in the middle of the night and can’t get back to sleep.   I start off saying, “O God, You are my Abba or my Advocate – Thank You for being so awesome! And then “God You are Beautiful and full of blessings – thank You I am your beloved child!” God – You are the Christ and full of compassion and You care for me – Thank You!”   I fill myself up with the goodness of God and find that I can go back to sleep so much easier.   Remembering who God is and how He works in my life gives me hope and strength – and a peace to be able to rest.

 

Finally, David says that he is going to cling to God’s hand – and believe that God will hold him up during this crisis.   He found refuge and shelter underneath the shadow of God’s wings – and He was able to stay steady in the midst of the storm because he knew God was holding him and would carry him through.   You know I think that is the only way I can get through times when I am going through the wilderness – I have to again and again tell myself – no matter what happens, God is there and He will hold me up and carry me through, no matter what.  And He will give me wisdom and strength to work out what ever problems I am facing – knowing He is right there with me to guide me.

 

I don’t know where you are today.   Maybe you are right in the midst of the wilderness – and feeling empty and alone.   If not, I am sure there will be times in the future when you might just find yourself in the middle of a muddle – in those unsettled and confusing times.   May I encourage you with the good news of Scripture that you are never alone!  God is there right in the middle of it all and His love can quench the thirsting of your soul.   Earnestly seek Him –Praise Him, in whatever your circumstances– Remember who He Is – and cling to His hand.   He will lead You through the wilderness and never let you go.
