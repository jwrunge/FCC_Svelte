At the Crossroads
Obedience

 

    By Pastor Martha-Jean Fitch

Plus, a Sermon Dialogue with the Apostle John

By Ted Schroeder. © 2016 Creative Communications for the Parish,  www.creativecommunications.com.
Text:  Matthew 26:36-46

 

 

 

36 Then Jesus went with them to the olive grove called Gethsemane, and he said, “Sit here while I go over there to pray.” 37 He took Peter and Zebedee’s two sons, James and John, and he became anguished and distressed. 38 He told them, “My soul is crushed with grief to the point of death. Stay here and keep watch with me.”  39 He went on a little farther and bowed with his face to the ground, praying, “My Father! If it is possible, let this cup of suffering be taken away from me. Yet I want your will to be done, not mine.” 40 Then he returned to the disciples and found them asleep. He said to Peter, “Couldn’t you watch with me even one hour? 41 Keep watch and pray, so that you will not give in to temptation. For the spirit is willing, but the body is weak!”

42 Then Jesus left them a second time and prayed, “My Father! If this cup cannot be taken away[a] unless I drink it, your will be done.” 43 When he returned to them again, he found them sleeping, for they couldn’t keep their eyes open. 44 So he went to pray a third time, saying the same things again. 45 Then he came to the disciples and said, “Go ahead and sleep. Have your rest. But look—the time has come. The Son of Man is betrayed into the hands of sinners. 46 Up, let’s be going. Look, my betrayer is here!”

 

Crossroads.   They are there for all of us.   We each face them from time to time.   A crossroads is a place where a decision has to be made.   Do I take this road or that?   Do I turn to the right or turn to the left?   Do I spend my time doing here or there?   Do I spend my money on this or that?   Some of those crossroads can be simple and easy and the answer is very clear.   But some of those crossroads are critical – and the decisions we make can bring far reaching consequences.   The decisions we make can have a major impact on our lives and on the people around us.  And ultimately, some of the crossroads that are faced are a matter of life or death.  

 

When standing at a crossroad, the question we are called to ask is:  what do you want me to do God?   What is your will?   And then, “will I obey You, especially if is it difficult, demanding or costly?”

 

            Today, we have an expert on the crossroad of obedience.   He was there in the Garden of Gethsemane when Jesus was faced that crossroad – when he confronted His part in God’s plan of salvation.  

 

We welcome the disciple John, the disciples Jesus loved.

 

JOHN ENTERS

 

Pastor:           John, welcome. Can you tell us something about that night in the Garden, what you saw and what you learned about Jesus?

 

John:  I’m almost embarrassed to be here. Yes, I was in the Garden with Jesus, but I certainly didn’t understand what was going on and I certainly was no help to him there.

 

Pastor:            Did you see him praying?

 

John:  Yes, he went away to pray. And he asked us to pray with him. I could tell he was struggling, but after the meal, after the cups of wine we drank with the Passover meal, after the walk to the Garden in the dark, I just couldn’t keep my eyes open. I admit I fell asleep.

 

Pastor:            Didn’t you understand the terror Jesus was facing?

 

John:  Not really. Not then. It was not unusual for us to go off as a group and join Jesus in prayer. It happened often. Jesus was busy at prayer many times as we traveled with him. I just didn’t know what was going on there in the Garden.

 

Pastor:  Do you understand now?

 

John:              Now. Certainly. I wish I could go back to the garden and do it over. I wish I could join Jesus in his prayer, in his struggle. I know that it would be different now. Jesus was facing the most difficult crossroad in his life. He was staring at the reality of the suffering he would have to endure, facing the cross and most terribly, facing it alone.

 

Pastor:  Was it really different from the other times he had to choose between his own way and God’s way? Was his choice to obey in spite of the pain so unusual?

 

John:  Not really. We know that Jesus was often tempted in his life to step away from God’s plan for him, God’s plan for us. Satan harassed him like he had done in the wilderness with a way out, a way to avoid the suffering. “Just bow down and worship me,” Satan said. “And I will give all this to you.” He was offering a shortcut that avoided the cross, but Jesus sent him away and clung to the path that God had set for him.

 

Pastor:  What does Jesus’ choice to obey, in spite of the terror that faced him, mean to you now?

 

John:  It inspires me and convicts me. It must do the same for you.

 

Pastor:  How do you mean?

 

John:              I’m certainly not unique. I faced a crossroad like Jesus’ many times in my life. Oh, not with the weight of the world on my shoulders, but with my own heart in conflict over which way to go. I faced that crossroad every time I came to the place where I heard the voice in me say: “Look, you know what will happen if you choose to obey God’s claim on your life, you will be hurt, you will be excluded, you will be scorned, you will be persecuted.    Every time I stood at the crossroad, I remembered Jesus in the garden and asked him to stay awake with me and watch with me and pray for me.

 

Pastor:   Did that happen often?

John:  In those years after Jesus ascended, all of us who followed him were faced with the choice to follow or deny him. If we denied Him, we could escape the threats, the persecution, even the death that faced us. We were outcast, rejected by many of our brothers, and threatened often with suffering or death. I asked God’s help to remain faithful and encouraged others to do the same in my book called Revelation. I wrote: “Do not fear what you are about to suffer. Behold, the devil is about to throw some of you into prison, that you may be tested, and for ten days you will have tribulation. Be faithful unto death, and I will give you the crown of life.” (Rev. 2:10).

 

Pastor:   But we aren’t persecuted. We don’t face that kind of crossroad.

 

John: You face the crossroad of obedience every time you confront a choice that asks will you follow your own way, the way of the world, the way that others push you to, the way you think will be easiest or most pleasant for you. Or will you follow the way Jesus would lead you – the way of obedience.

 

Pastor:   But those kinds of choices happen every day.

 

John:  And that is why you need the power and presence of God’s Spirit every day. That’s what Jesus was doing there in the Garden and on the cross. He was bringing forgiveness and new life to you there. By God’s power and the sacrifice of Jesus, you begin each day, new and forgiven, new and able to make the choice of obedience.

 

Pastor:    And how can we make those right choices?

 

John:    Here’s another thing I wrote in the my Gospel, Jesus said: “Abide in me, and I in you” (John 15:4). The same Jesus who struggled there in the Garden, the one who went to the cross, the one who rose, is there in our hearts, with us at our crossroads and in our choices.

 

Pastor:     Thank you, John, for those words of encouragement. We will remember with you and rejoice with you for what Jesus as done and what he will do in us and through us.

 

              As Jesus went to the Garden of Gethsemane, He was facing the most difficult crossroad of His life.   And He knew He needed to be in prayer to His heavenly Father as He stood at that crossroad.   He took His disciples with Him – in particular Peter, James and John – and said, “My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death.  Stay here and watch and pray that you will not fall into temptation.  The spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak.”  But they did fall into temptation – and disobeyed His command to watch and pray.   They chose the path of comfort and sleep – right when Jesus was in anguish and agony and needed their support and prayer.   

 

            But Jesus chose the way of obedience.   In His time of prayer, He cried out to God that if it were possible, that the cup be taken away from Him.   He wrestled and struggled, even to the point to sweating drops of blood – but then, He said the words that changed everything – “Not my will but Yours be done.”   

 

            God didn’t answer the prayer by taking the cup away from Jesus.   But Jesus knew that God had a greater plan – and He could leave it in God’s hands.   He had to obey His heavenly Father and go the path He had planned for Him.   God could see beyond the pain that was coming to His Son – He could see beyond to the resurrection and how He would use this incredible sacrifice of His Son for the salvation of the world.

 

            How often in our lives have we prayed for God to change some circumstance or problem and we haven’t gotten the answer we were wanting. And we didn’t know that God may indeed have a bigger plan – a plan that can, in long run bring a greater good?   How often have we prayed and not liked the answer we received – so we decided not to obey God’s word to us – and we were miserable?

 

            The prayer that works every time – if prayed with full sincerity and obedience – is the prayer of Jesus: “Not my will but Yours be done.”   Those seven words and our obedience to answer what God tells us – can change everything!   It will not always lead to an easy, care-free life – but may actually lead to something hard or costly.   But God will redeem and use everything we go through and give us the power and strength to carry out His will.

 

            This Lent, all of us are standing at a crossroads that we did not anticipate or see coming.   Every one of us is standing at the same crossroads – and the question is “how are we going to respond”.  Can we actually pray, “God, not my will but Yours be done” and obey?

 

            Each of us were going on our own path and direction as we began our journey of Lent – however the pandemic has changed everything.   Unexpected measures have been placed upon us:   social distancing, cancelling of work, stay at home orders, and even the closing of church and entertainment and eating options.   Our world has turned upside down. As one commentator has said, “Far beyond our control, God’s permissive will has allowed the pestilence of the coronavirus to cover the earth.”1   We cry out to God to bring an end to the pandemic and we don’t understand how it just seems to be getting bigger and broader in its attack.   I think we don’t really know and understand completely how God is at work in all of this crisis – and how we can be changed and blessed even through it all.

 

            We have a choice as we stand at this crossroad.   We have a choice at how we are going to respond.   As Father James Kirby has said: “We can be angry, shake our fists, annoy our neighbors, and burden our loved ones with complaining and negativity, or we can allow God’s grace to take us above such things. We can choose a higher path.”2 We can pray for God’s will to be done – and the obey Him in and through it all.

 

         So here are just a few of the choices I think God is calling us to make through these days of crisis:

 

1.    Choose faith instead of fear.  I believe that all this uncertainty and distress is designed by the enemy to cause us to fear.   But the promise from Scripture in II Timothy is that God did not give us a spirit of fear but of power and love and self-discipline.   And as we seek the Lord (Psalm 34), He will deliver us from all our fears.

 

2.    Choose to trust and give up control.   This crisis can make us crazy because we have no control.   I heard someone recently share that that is why we have such a hoarding of toilet paper and bread – because people feel like that is one way, they can have some control.   But we have to realize – we aren’t in control – God is.  Our prayers need to be asking for His will to be done in all of this – and that we can learn and grow through this crisis.   And we have to believe that just as God has been our help in ages past – He can and will deliver us from our problems today. 

 

3.    Choose peace over anxiety.   We are called to not be anxious about anything but to pray about everything – “so that the peace that passes all understanding” will rest upon us.   Instead of stewing and fretting and filling yourself up with all the bad news – fill yourself up with the promises of God and pray.  Open yourself up to the moving of the Holy Spirit within you.   He will be your peace in the storm and your light in the darkness.

 

4.       Choose to be a blessing.   As Catherine Segars says, “As coronavirus threatens to destroy life and livelihood, it should bring out the best in us. Let it bring out the best in you. Let it unify us.  Viewed through the right lens, the coronavirus crisis can be a conduit of blessings to you and through you to others. Don’t miss those opportunities to grow and allow God’s blessings to flow.”3

Find ways you can truly show the love of your neighbor – and let Jesus shine through you.

 

May it be that as we stand at this crossroad of a worldwide crisis, that God’s will, will be done – and that we will be obedient to serve Him faithfully.
