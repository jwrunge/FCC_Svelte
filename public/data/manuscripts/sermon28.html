SHELTER
MY ROCK AND FORTRESS

    by Pastor Martha-Jean Fitch
June 7, 2020
Text:  Psalm 71

 

 

Sermon begins with a 1:46 minute sermon video entitled “What Hasn’t Changed” from sermoncentral.com.   The video emphasizes that with all the uncertainties and changes this year, there is one thing that has not changed:   The tomb is still empty, God’s hope and

strength are still there – and God is still in control.

 

            When we started the new year of 2020 – What were you expecting would happen?   What hopes and dreams did you have?   Isn’t it incredible how things have changed in our world since the beginning of a new year?   Every week, if not every day almost, it sees like there are a new set of problems and worries and concerns that happen.   The Covid 19 pandemic, masks and sheltering in place, 40 million unemployed and financial instability, growing division and hatred in politics and now, protests and riots over the horrible death of George Floyd.   Unrest and uncertainty.   I don’t know about you, but I feel overwhelmed at times – and weary.   Just watching a sampling of the news broadcasts can leave me feeling very unsettled, with a great heaviness of heart.   Especially when at times it is hard to discern what the truth is – or what the future will bring.  In our isolation through sheltering, it can be easy to fall into a state of depression or a spirit of fear or anxiety.

 

            King David is one who often had those same feelings.   Many of the psalms were prayers written by David to God or about God.   These psalms show the roller coaster of emotions David went through.   He didn’t hold anything back – but shared his deep pain – but also his depth of faith and hope.   He came to know that God was truly his shelter and strength – and with Him he could face the trials and sufferings in his life.   It was the unchanging God that He could put in to get through the various trials he faced in life.

 

            And that is going to be our focus for the next several weeks – as we look at the book of the Psalms.   We’ll look at how we can find hope and help through several powerful psalms – 7 to be exact.

 

And as we begin this new series, I have a challenge for you.   My challenge is that you begin reading all of the book of Psalms.   There are 150 Psalms.   And there are 208 days left in the year 2020.   Why not fill yourself up with the strength and hope of the Psalms – a message we so desperately need in this year of uncertainty.   

 

            Well, for today, we are going to be looking at Psalm 71.   We don’t know exactly who wrote this Psalm – but many believe it was written by David, when he was an older man, at a time when he was experiencing a lot of heartache.   His son, Abonijah was trying to take over the kingdom from his father.   Talk about being unsettled and worried – not knowing what the future will bring.  

 

            Well – David cries out to God – giving hints at what some of the problems were – but also claiming his faith and hope in God.   Let’s hear Tracy Jackson read the whole Psalm 71 for us now.   While she is reading the text, I would invite you to follow along in your own Bible.   Each time you hear a description of who God is – underline it in your Bible or circle it.

 

Tracy Jackson reads Psalm 71

 

            David needs protection from his enemies.   They are plotting to kill him.   And he is feeling his age… which may be part of the enemies that pursue him.   He is struggling and facing hardship.   Have you ever felt that way?   Have you ever just cried out to God – “God hurry to help me – don’t stay away or abandon me!”

 

            When I was a little girl, whenever I would have a bad dream – I would call out for my parents – or I would muster up my courage to run down the hall to their bedroom, to get their comfort.   I remember one night, very vividly – my mother had surgery and was in the hospital for several days when I was about 8.   I was so unsettled and worried, I couldn’t sleep.   I ran to my father, who held me in his arms until I settled down and fell asleep.   I felt so safe and secure in my father’s arms – and my fears and anxieties melted away.

 

            That is the way our heavenly Father wants us to feel about Him.   Just as David, came to his heavenly Father and poured out his heart to God – telling him his fear and needs.   God wants us to know that He is always near us – just a prayer away and we can feel safe and secure sheltered in the arms of God.

 

            In Psalm 71, David lays out all his fears and needs – but did you notice how many times David affirmed who God is as well.    One of the best ways to handle all these uncertainties and struggles in life – is to remember who God is – our rock and our fortress who is immovable, steadfast and faithful.   When everything around us seems questionable and it is hard to find the truth – we can trust God, who is our rock of safety and the truth we can depend on.     When we just don’t know what to think about current events and how we should respond – we can lean on Jesus to give us wisdom and discernment.  

 

            Our theme Scripture for our series from Psalm 91, tells us the “whoever dwells in the shelter of the Most High – will rest in the shadow of the Almighty.”   You will find shelter under His wings and there we will find refuge and strength.   That is our hope we cling to as an anchor in the midst of the storms.

 

            So – remember who God is, as you go through uncertain and difficult times.

 

            And remember how He helped you in the past.  David recalled how had trusted God since childhood and how God was always there to care for him.   God was faithful to David in the past, keeping His promises – and David could count on that to help him in his current crisis – and in the future.

 

            It reminds me of that old hymn, “O God our help in ages past, our hope in years to come!”   Think back – can you recall times when you could see God’s hand – helping you, getting you through some difficult and painful times?   I know for myself – in some of the trials that I have gone through – I can say without a doubt that God carried me through what at the time I thought was totally impossible.   And because I can remember those days, I can hope for the future – that whatever life brings me – I can get through it, because God is faithful and will carry me through.

 

            Remember who God is – remember how He has helped you in the past  - and then praise Him.   Several times throughout the psalm, David talks about praising God – telling of His glory and His faithfulness.  In fact, you could almost hear that hymn, “Rock of Ages” in those words – or Martin Luther’s hymn, “A Mighty Fortress is Our God” – that we sang earlier.   What is interesting is that Martin Luther struggled with depression – but he never lost his faith in Christ.   He knew he could count on God being his mighty fortress.1

 

            David Jeremiah has said that “Whenever you go through a trial, God will give you a verse and a song of praise to sustain you – a song to take you through the dark night.”2    Instead of rehearsing all those fears and worries and trials – fill yourself up with that verse or song of praise – and see if you don’t find God lifting your spirits and giving you strength for the journey.

 

            Remember who God is – remember how He has helped you in the past – sing His praises – and then: proclaim His story to others.   David prays that he wants to tell everyone the wonderful things God has done and proclaim God’s power to the next generation.   David wants his heritage to be one of faith and trust in God – a legacy he can pass on to those who come after him.

 

            I believe throughout this pandemic and through the racial unrest – as well as all the political divisiveness that is prevalent – we have seen all sorts of examples of how we are not to live.   God wants us to look to Him for strength and power – and to love Him first and foremost – and ALL our neighbors, and yes, even our enemies.  God wants us to look out and care for the least among us – even to the point of sacrificing our comfort and our own desires.  And God wants us to be peacemakers and reconcilers.     People will be looking to see how we handle these struggles.   Can we be a witness of our faithful and righteous God?   Can we be, as Tony Evans says, “Part of the solution and not just part of the complaint”?  

 

            What can you do to proclaim God’s praises – and point to how God is at work in your life?   What testimony can you give, like David did, of how God sheltered you and carried you through dark and difficult days.

 

            Father Michael Joncas, is a priest and well-known writer of great hymns of the faith.   In fact, he wrote the hymn you will hear Steve play during communion today, “On Eagle’s Wings”.   In response to the spread of Covid 19, Father Joncas has written a prayer song, entitled “Shelter Me”, which is going to be our theme song for this summer.

 

            Father Joncas says he awoke at 3am, March 26 and knew he wanted to write a song in response to the pandemic.  It became clear that he wanted to write a paraphrase of Psalm 23 – and by 10am the next day, the song was done.   The very next day, one of his publishers agreed to get the song out, on a rush job, so that all of us would be blessed by it, during these uncertain days.

 

            In it, he remembers past times of intimacy with God – and looks forward to the future hope with God.   And then in the middle of the text, he speaks of the valley of the shadow of death – which he felt spoke well about the times we are in.

 

            This was Father Joncas’ way to praise God – and proclaim the hope we have in Him.   His words leave a legacy to us of faith – “All will be well as we shelter in God”.3

 

            Listen for God speaking to you, as you start to learn this song – and as you read through the psalms.   May you come to know that He is your rock and fortress that will hold you steadfast in the mist of our changing world.
